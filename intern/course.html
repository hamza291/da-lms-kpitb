1.1 — What is Data Analytics?

⌄

What You Will Learn

Data analytics is the process of examining raw data to find patterns, answer questions, and support decision-making. It is not just about numbers — it is about turning data into insight and insight into action.

The 4 Types of Analytics

Every analytics task falls into one of four categories. Think of them as a ladder — each level builds on the one before it.

PRESCRIPTIVE   → What should we DO?           (highest value)
     ↑
PREDICTIVE     → What will HAPPEN?
     ↑
DIAGNOSTIC     → WHY did it happen?
     ↑
DESCRIPTIVE    → What HAPPENED?               (most common)

1. Descriptive Analytics

Answers: "What happened?"

This is the foundation of all analytics. It summarizes historical data using statistics, charts, and dashboards.

  • *Example*: A monthly sales report showing revenue by region.
  • *Tools*: Excel, Power BI, SQL (COUNT, SUM, AVG)

2. Diagnostic Analytics

Answers: "Why did it happen?"

This goes deeper to find the root cause of a trend or event using drill-downs, filtering, and comparison.

  • *Example*: Investigating why sales dropped 20% in Lahore in March.
  • *Tools*: SQL (subqueries, joins), Excel (pivot tables)

3. Predictive Analytics

Answers: "What is likely to happen?"

Uses historical patterns and statistical/ML models to forecast future outcomes.

  • *Example*: Predicting which customers are likely to churn in the next 30 days.
  • *Tools*: Python (scikit-learn), R, Excel trend lines

4. Prescriptive Analytics

Answers: "What should we do about it?"

The most advanced type — provides actionable recommendations using optimization and simulation.

  • *Example*: Recommending the optimal discount level to maximize revenue while minimizing churn.
  • *Tools*: Advanced ML, Operations Research models
NOTE: Most internship-level analysts work at the Descriptive and Diagnostic levels. Mastering these two will make you immediately valuable.

1.2 — The Data Lifecycle

⌄

Every analytics project follows the same pipeline. Understanding this gives you the "big picture" before you dive into individual tools.

1. COLLECT    → Gather raw data from sources (databases, APIs, forms, sensors)
2. CLEAN      → Remove errors, duplicates, fix formatting, handle missing values
3. EXPLORE    → Understand the data — distributions, relationships, anomalies (EDA)
4. ANALYZE    → Apply statistics, aggregations, or models to answer questions
5. VISUALIZE  → Create charts and dashboards to communicate findings
6. DECIDE     → Stakeholders use insights to make better decisions

Key Principle: Analysts spend ~80% of their time on steps 1–3 (collecting and cleaning). Clean data is the foundation of every good analysis.

Real Example: A government ministry wants to track hospital performance.

1. Collect: Pull patient records from 50 hospitals into one database

2. Clean: Fix missing patient IDs, remove duplicate entries, standardize dates

3. Explore: Check distribution of wait times, identify outlier hospitals

4. Analyze: Compare average wait time by hospital, calculate bed occupancy rates

5. Visualize: Build a dashboard showing performance by hospital and region

6. Decide: Ministry redirects resources to 5 underperforming hospitals

1.3 — KPIs vs Metrics

⌄

These two terms are often confused. Here is the distinction:

Metric: Any measurable value tracked over time.

  • *Examples*: Number of website visitors, number of complaints received, total revenue

KPI (Key Performance Indicator): A specific metric directly tied to a strategic business goal.

  • *Examples*: Monthly Active Users (if growth is the goal), Customer Satisfaction Score (if service is the goal)

The SMART KPI Framework:

A good KPI must be:

  • Specific — clearly defined
  • Measurable — tracked with a number
  • Achievable — realistic given resources
  • Relevant — tied to a business goal
  • Time-bound — measured over a defined period

Example of a bad KPI: "Improve customer experience" ← vague, unmeasurable

Example of a good KPI: "Achieve a Net Promoter Score (NPS) ≥ 60 by Q4 2026" ← SMART

Leading vs Lagging Indicators:

  • Leading indicator: Measures input/activity → *predicts* future performance
  • *Example*: Number of sales calls made this week (predicts future revenue)
  • Lagging indicator: Measures the *result* after the fact
  • *Example*: Total revenue earned last quarter

⌄

Understanding data types is critical because the type of data determines which analysis you can do.

Structured vs Unstructured:

Type Definition Examples
Structured Organized in rows and columns Excel files, SQL tables, CSV
Semi-structured Has some organization but not rigid JSON, XML, emails
Unstructured No predefined format Text, images, video, audio

Quantitative vs Qualitative:

Type Description Examples
Quantitative (Numerical) Numbers you can do math on Revenue, Age, Temperature
Qualitative (Categorical) Labels or categories Department name, City, Status

The NOIR Scale (memorize this):

Level Type Key Property Example
Nominal Categories with no order Just labels Gender, Color, Department
Ordinal Categories with order Rank matters, gaps don't Rating (1–5 stars), Rank
Interval Numbers with equal gaps, no true zero Math (+ −) valid Temperature in Celsius
Ratio Numbers with true zero All math valid Height, Weight, Revenue
TIP: The type of data determines the type of chart and statistical test you can use. Always identify data types before starting any analysis.

1.5 — Data Ethics

⌄

Data analysts handle sensitive information. Professional ethics is not optional.

Key Principles:

1. Privacy: Collect only the data you need. Protect personally identifiable information (PII) such as names, ID numbers, and health records.

2. Consent: Users should know their data is being collected and agree to it. Violating this is illegal under laws like GDPR.

3. Bias: If your dataset is not representative, your analysis will be misleading.

  • *Example*: A hiring algorithm trained only on male engineers will discriminate against women.

4. Transparency: Be honest about the limitations of your data and analysis. Never cherry-pick data to tell a story you want.

5. Accountability: Analysts are responsible for the quality and accuracy of their work. Wrong analysis leads to wrong decisions.

Real Case: In 2018, Cambridge Analytica harvested Facebook data of 87 million users without consent for political targeting. This led to massive fines and global privacy law reforms. As a data analyst, you must never participate in practices like this.

2.2 — Finance & Accounting for Analysts

⌄

You do not need to be an accountant — but you must read and understand financial data.

The 3 Core Financial Statements

1. Income Statement (Profit & Loss)

Answers: Did we make money this period?

Revenue (Sales)                   PKR 10,000,000
– Cost of Goods Sold (COGS)      PKR  4,000,000
= GROSS PROFIT                    PKR  6,000,000   ← Gross Margin = 60%
– Operating Expenses              PKR  3,000,000
= OPERATING INCOME (EBIT)         PKR  3,000,000
– Interest & Tax                  PKR    800,000
= NET PROFIT                      PKR  2,200,000   ← Net Margin = 22%

2. Balance Sheet

Answers: What does the company own and owe RIGHT NOW?

  • Assets = What you own (cash, buildings, equipment, receivables)
  • Liabilities = What you owe (loans, payables, debt)
  • Equity = Assets – Liabilities (owner's stake in the business)
  • *Golden rule*: Assets = Liabilities + Equity (always)

3. Cash Flow Statement

Answers: Where did the money actually come from and go?

Profit ≠ Cash. A company can be profitable but still run out of cash. This statement tracks actual cash movement.

Key Financial KPIs

KPI Formula What It Tells You
Gross Margin % Gross Profit / Revenue × 100 Profitability after production cost
Net Profit Margin % Net Profit / Revenue × 100 Overall profitability
ROI (Net Profit / Investment) × 100 Return on every rupee invested
CAC Total Marketing Spend / New Customers Cost to acquire one customer
LTV (CLV) Avg Purchase Value × Frequency × Lifespan Total value of a customer
LTV:CAC Ratio LTV / CAC Sustainability of growth (target: ≥ 3:1)
Break-even Point Fixed Costs / (Price – Variable Cost per unit) When you start making profit

Example: If CAC = PKR 2,000 and LTV = PKR 10,000, your LTV:CAC = 5:1 — excellent. If LTV:CAC = 1.5:1, you're losing money per customer long-term.

2.3 — Marketing Analytics

⌄

The Marketing Funnel

Customers don't buy immediately. They go through stages:

AWARENESS    → They discover your brand (impressions, reach)
CONSIDERATION → They evaluate your product (website visits, demos)
CONVERSION   → They make a purchase (transactions, sign-ups)
RETENTION    → They come back and stay (repeat purchases, subscriptions)
ADVOCACY     → They recommend you (reviews, referrals, NPS)

Each stage has specific metrics:

Funnel Stage Key Metrics
Awareness Impressions, Reach, Brand Recall
Consideration Click-Through Rate (CTR), Time on Page, Bounce Rate
Conversion Conversion Rate, Cost Per Acquisition (CPA)
Retention Churn Rate, Repeat Purchase Rate, NPS

Essential Marketing KPIs

CTR (Click-Through Rate)

CTR = (Clicks / Impressions) × 100
Example: 500 clicks / 20,000 impressions = 2.5% CTR

Conversion Rate

Conversion Rate = (Conversions / Visitors) × 100
Example: 150 purchases / 5,000 visitors = 3% conversion rate

ROAS (Return on Ad Spend)

ROAS = Revenue from Ads / Ad Spend
Example: PKR 800,000 revenue / PKR 200,000 spend = 4x ROAS (every PKR 1 spent returned PKR 4)

NPS (Net Promoter Score)

Customers are asked: "How likely are you to recommend us (0–10)?"

  • Promoters: 9–10
  • Passives: 7–8
  • Detractors: 0–6
    • NPS = % Promoters – % Detractors

Scores above 50 are considered excellent.

RFM Analysis

A powerful customer segmentation technique using 3 dimensions:

  • Recency: How recently did the customer buy?
  • Frequency: How often do they buy?
  • Monetary: How much do they spend?

Customers are scored 1–5 on each dimension. High R, F, M = "Champions". Low on all = "Lost Customers".

3.1 — Descriptive Statistics

⌄

Descriptive statistics summarize and describe the main features of a dataset. Every analysis begins here.

Measures of Central Tendency

Where is the "center" of the data?

Mean (Average)

Mean = Sum of all values / Number of values
Dataset: [10, 20, 30, 40, 50]  →  Mean = 150/5 = 30

Problem: Mean is sensitive to outliers.

Example: If one employee earns PKR 5,000,000 and 9 earn PKR 50,000 — the mean salary is PKR 545,000, which misrepresents the typical employee.

Median

The middle value when sorted. Not affected by outliers.

Dataset: [10, 20, 30, 40, 50]  →  Median = 30
Dataset: [10, 20, 30, 40]      →  Median = (20+30)/2 = 25

Rule of thumb: Use median for skewed data (salaries, house prices, wealth).

Mode

The most frequently occurring value. Used for categorical data.

Dataset: [Red, Blue, Red, Green, Red]  →  Mode = Red

Measures of Spread (Dispersion)

How spread out is the data?

Range

Range = Maximum – Minimum
Dataset: [10, 20, 30, 40, 100]  →  Range = 100 – 10 = 90

Variance & Standard Deviation

Variance measures the average squared distance from the mean. Standard Deviation (SD) is its square root — in the original unit.

Dataset: [2, 4, 4, 4, 5, 5, 7, 9]
Mean = 5
Variance = [(2-5)² + (4-5)² + ... ] / n = 4
Std Dev = √4 = 2

Interpretation: SD = 2 means most values are within 2 units of the mean.

The 68-95-99.7 Rule (Normal Distribution):

  • ~68% of values fall within 1 SD of mean
  • ~95% of values fall within 2 SD of mean
  • ~99.7% of values fall within 3 SD of mean

IQR (Interquartile Range)

IQR = Q3 – Q1

Where:

  • Q1 = 25th percentile (lower quarter)
  • Q2 = 50th percentile (Median)
  • Q3 = 75th percentile (upper quarter)

Outlier Detection using IQR:

Lower Fence = Q1 – 1.5 × IQR
Upper Fence = Q3 + 1.5 × IQR
Any value outside these fences = outlier

Distribution Shape

Normal Distribution (Bell Curve)

The most important distribution in statistics. Mean = Median = Mode. Symmetric around the center.

Skewness

Right-Skewed (+): Long tail on the RIGHT → Mean > Median
                  Common in: income, sales, house prices
Left-Skewed (−):  Long tail on the LEFT  → Mean < Median
                  Common in: age at retirement, exam scores close to 100

3.2 — Probability Fundamentals

⌄

Probability is the mathematics of uncertainty. Every prediction, hypothesis test, and risk assessment is built on probability.

Basic Rules

Range: Probability is always between 0 (impossible) and 1 (certain).

Addition Rule (OR):

P(A or B) = P(A) + P(B) – P(A and B)

If A and B can't happen together (mutually exclusive):

P(A or B) = P(A) + P(B)

Multiplication Rule (AND):

P(A and B) = P(A) × P(B)   [if A and B are independent]

Conditional Probability

The probability of A given that B has already happened.

P(A|B) = P(A and B) / P(B)

Example: P(customer buys | they opened the email) = 0.15. Means 15% of email openers make a purchase.

Bayes' Theorem

Updates probability based on new evidence. Used in spam filters, medical testing, and fraud detection.

P(A|B) = [P(B|A) × P(A)] / P(B)

Intuitive example: A medical test for a disease is 99% accurate. But if the disease is rare (1 in 10,000 people), a positive test still has only ~1% chance of being correct. Bayes explains why.

Key Distributions

Distribution Type When to Use Example
Normal Continuous Heights, exam scores, measurement errors Average employee performance
Binomial Discrete Success/failure over n trials Number of defective items in a batch of 100
Poisson Discrete Count of events in a fixed period Number of calls to a helpdesk per hour
Uniform Continuous All outcomes equally likely Random number generation

3.3 — Inferential Statistics & Hypothesis Testing

⌄

Descriptive stats describe your sample. Inferential stats make conclusions about the larger population.

Population vs Sample

POPULATION: All customers in Pakistan (millions — too expensive to survey all)
    ↓
SAMPLE: 1,000 randomly selected customers (affordable, if done correctly)
    ↓
INFERENCE: Use sample to make conclusions about the whole population

Hypothesis Testing Framework

A formal procedure to test a claim using data.

Step 1: Define hypotheses

H₀ (Null Hypothesis):      "There is NO effect / no difference"
H₁ (Alternative Hypothesis): "There IS an effect / a difference"

Step 2: Choose significance level (α)

  • Standard: α = 0.05 (5% risk of being wrong)

Step 3: Collect data and calculate the test statistic

Step 4: Find the p-value

  • p-value = Probability of seeing your result (or more extreme) IF H₀ is true

Step 5: Make a decision

If p-value < α (0.05)  → REJECT H₀  → Result is statistically significant
If p-value ≥ α (0.05)  → FAIL TO REJECT H₀ → Not enough evidence

Error Types

H₀ is Actually True H₀ is Actually False
Reject H₀ Type I Error (False Positive) ❌ Correct Decision ✅
Fail to Reject H₀ Correct Decision ✅ Type II Error (False Negative) ❌

Example: Testing whether a new training program improved employee performance:

  • H₀: Training had no effect on performance
  • H₁: Training improved performance
  • If p = 0.02 (< 0.05) → Reject H₀ → Training DID improve performance

Common Statistical Tests

Test When to Use Example
One-sample t-test Compare sample mean to a known value Is average salary = PKR 60,000?
Two-sample t-test Compare means of two groups Do men and women have different avg salaries?
Chi-Square test Test relationship between two categorical variables Is job satisfaction related to department?
ANOVA Compare means across 3+ groups Do 4 regional offices have different avg productivity?
Pearson Correlation Measure linear relationship between two numeric variables Does marketing spend correlate with sales?

Correlation

r = +1.0   → Perfect positive correlation
r = +0.7   → Strong positive correlation
r = +0.3   → Weak positive correlation
r =  0.0   → No correlation
r = -0.7   → Strong negative correlation
r = -1.0   → Perfect negative correlation

⚠️ CRITICAL: Correlation ≠ Causation

Ice cream sales and drowning deaths both increase in summer. They are correlated, but ice cream does NOT cause drowning. The hidden cause is hot weather. Always look for confounding variables.

3.4 — Data Quality

⌄

Bad data leads to bad decisions. Every analyst must understand what makes data good or bad.

The 6 Dimensions of Data Quality

Dimension Definition Bad Example
Accuracy Data correctly represents reality Age = 150 years
Completeness All required data is present 30% of salary values are blank
Consistency Same data matches across systems "IT" in one table, "I.T." in another
Timeliness Data is up-to-date Using 2020 data to make 2026 decisions
Validity Data conforms to defined rules Email field contains a phone number
Uniqueness No unintended duplicate records Same employee appears 3 times

Missing Data — Types & Treatments

Types of Missingness:

  • MCAR (Missing Completely At Random): Randomly missing, no pattern. Safe to delete.
  • MAR (Missing At Random): Missing related to other observed data. Impute carefully.
  • MNAR (Missing Not At Random): Missing because of the value itself (e.g., high earners skip salary question). Most dangerous — requires domain knowledge.

Treatment Strategies:

Strategy When to Use
Drop rows MCAR, <5% missing, deletion won't bias
Fill with mean Numerical data, symmetric distribution
Fill with median Numerical data, skewed distribution
Fill with mode Categorical data
Model-based imputation Complex, large datasets (advanced)

Outlier Detection

Z-Score Method:

Z = (Value – Mean) / Standard Deviation
Rule: |Z| > 3  →  outlier

IQR Method:

Lower fence = Q1 – 1.5 × IQR
Upper fence = Q3 + 1.5 × IQR

Treatment:

  • Remove: If clearly erroneous (age = -5)
  • Cap/Floor (Winsorize): Replace with fence value — keeps the row, reduces distortion
  • Keep and note: If a genuine extreme value with business meaning

3.5 — Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA)

⌄

EDA is the process of getting to know your dataset before drawing conclusions. The term was coined by statistician John Tukey in 1977.

EDA Workflow

STEP 1: Understand structure
         → How many rows/columns? What are the data types?
         → df.shape, df.info(), df.dtypes

STEP 2: Summary statistics
         → What are the ranges, averages, distributions?
         → df.describe()

STEP 3: Univariate Analysis
         → Analyze each variable on its own
         → Histogram (continuous), Bar chart (categorical)
         → Check for skewness, outliers, unexpected values

STEP 4: Bivariate Analysis
         → Analyze relationships between pairs of variables
         → Scatter plot (2 continuous), Box plot (1 cat + 1 cont)
         → Correlation matrix

STEP 5: Multivariate Analysis
         → Look for interactions between 3+ variables
         → Heatmaps, Pair plots, Grouped charts

STEP 6: Formulate hypotheses
         → Based on patterns found, write testable hypotheses
         → "Regions with higher ad spend seem to have higher sales — let's test this"

Key EDA Questions (Always Ask These)

1. What is the shape of this dataset? (rows × columns)

2. What are the data types of each column?

3. How many missing values are there, and where?

4. Are there any obvious outliers?

5. What is the distribution of each numeric variable?

6. Are there any surprising patterns or relationships?

7. Are there any obvious data quality issues?

3.6 — Analytics Frameworks

⌄

These structured thinking tools help you turn business problems into data problems.

SWOT Analysis

Used to assess a situation before designing an analytics project.

  • Strengths: What data/capabilities do we have?
  • Weaknesses: Where are our gaps?
  • Opportunities: What insights could drive growth?
  • Threats: What risks do we face?

Root Cause Analysis — 5 Whys

Ask "Why?" 5 times to drill down to the true cause.

Problem: Sales dropped 25% in Karachi
Why 1? → Fewer customers visited the website
Why 2? → Social media traffic dropped
Why 3? → Our Facebook ads were paused
Why 4? → Budget was cut by 40%
Why 5? → Finance department froze discretionary spending
Root Cause: Budget freeze → fix by creating an exception process for marketing spend

Pareto Principle (80/20 Rule)

80% of effects come from 20% of causes.

  • *Example*: 20% of products generate 80% of revenue
  • *Application*: Focus your analysis on the 20% that matters most

The Analytical Thinking Process

PROBLEM → QUESTION → HYPOTHESIS → DATA → ANALYSIS → INSIGHT → DECISION

5.2 — Basic SQL Queries

⌄

SELECT — retrieve data

-- Get all columns from the Employees table
SELECT * FROM Employees;

-- Get specific columns
SELECT Name, Department, Salary FROM Employees;

-- Remove duplicates
SELECT DISTINCT Department FROM Employees;

WHERE — filter rows

-- Employees earning more than PKR 70,000
SELECT Name, Salary FROM Employees WHERE Salary > 70000;

-- Multiple conditions
SELECT Name, Salary FROM Employees
WHERE Department = 'IT' AND Salary > 60000;

-- Range
SELECT Name FROM Employees WHERE HireDate BETWEEN '2023-01-01' AND '2025-12-31';

-- Pattern matching
SELECT Name FROM Employees WHERE Name LIKE 'A%';  -- names starting with A

ORDER BY — sort results

SELECT Name, Salary FROM Employees ORDER BY Salary DESC;  -- highest first
SELECT Name, Salary FROM Employees ORDER BY Salary ASC;   -- lowest first

Aggregation Functions

SELECT
    COUNT(*)        AS TotalEmployees,
    SUM(Salary)     AS TotalPayroll,
    AVG(Salary)     AS AverageSalary,
    MAX(Salary)     AS HighestSalary,
    MIN(Salary)     AS LowestSalary
FROM Employees;

GROUP BY — aggregate by category

-- Total salary per department
SELECT Department, SUM(Salary) AS TotalSalary
FROM Employees
GROUP BY Department;

HAVING — filter after grouping

-- Only departments where average salary > PKR 60,000
SELECT Department, AVG(Salary) AS AvgSalary
FROM Employees
GROUP BY Department
HAVING AVG(Salary) > 60000;
NOTE: WHERE vs HAVING: WHERE filters rows before grouping. HAVING filters groups after aggregation.

5.3 — SQL Joins

⌄

Joins combine rows from two or more tables based on a matching column.

INNER JOIN: Only matching rows from BOTH tables
LEFT JOIN:  All rows from LEFT + matching from right (NULL if no match)
RIGHT JOIN: All rows from RIGHT + matching from left
FULL JOIN:  All rows from both (NULL where no match)
-- INNER JOIN: Employees with their department names
SELECT e.Name, d.DeptName, e.Salary
FROM Employees e
INNER JOIN Departments d ON e.DeptID = d.DeptID;

-- LEFT JOIN: All employees, even those without a department
SELECT e.Name, d.DeptName
FROM Employees e
LEFT JOIN Departments d ON e.DeptID = d.DeptID;

5.4 — Advanced SQL

⌄

Subqueries

A query inside another query.

-- Employees earning more than the company average
SELECT Name, Salary
FROM Employees
WHERE Salary > (SELECT AVG(Salary) FROM Employees);

-- Top sales employee in each region
SELECT Region, MAX(TotalSales) AS MaxSales
FROM (
    SELECT EmployeeID, Region, SUM(Amount) AS TotalSales
    FROM Sales
    GROUP BY EmployeeID, Region
) AS RegionalSales
GROUP BY Region;

CTEs (Common Table Expressions)

A named temporary result set. Makes complex queries readable.

-- Using a CTE to find top 5 employees by sales
WITH EmployeeSales AS (
    SELECT e.Name, SUM(s.Amount) AS TotalSales
    FROM Employees e
    JOIN Sales s ON e.EmployeeID = s.EmployeeID
    GROUP BY e.Name
)
SELECT Name, TotalSales
FROM EmployeeSales
ORDER BY TotalSales DESC
LIMIT 5;

Window Functions

Perform calculations across a "window" of related rows without collapsing them like GROUP BY.

-- Rank employees by salary within each department
SELECT
    Name,
    Department,
    Salary,
    RANK() OVER (PARTITION BY Department ORDER BY Salary DESC) AS SalaryRank
FROM Employees;

-- Running total of sales
SELECT
    SaleDate,
    Amount,
    SUM(Amount) OVER (ORDER BY SaleDate) AS RunningTotal
FROM Sales;

-- Compare each month's sales to previous month
SELECT
    Month,
    TotalSales,
    LAG(TotalSales, 1) OVER (ORDER BY Month) AS PrevMonthSales,
    TotalSales - LAG(TotalSales, 1) OVER (ORDER BY Month) AS Growth
FROM MonthlySales;

7.1 — Choosing the Right Chart

⌄

The wrong chart destroys a good insight. This is the chart selection guide:

Data Situation Best Chart Avoid
Trend over time Line Chart Bar chart for long time series
Comparing categories Bar Chart (vertical or horizontal) Pie chart with many slices
Part of a whole (≤5 categories) Pie / Donut Chart Pie chart with >6 slices
Distribution of one variable Histogram Bar chart (for distribution)
Relationship between 2 numeric vars Scatter Plot Line chart
Distribution across categories Box Plot Bar chart of means only
Correlation of many variables Heatmap Multiple separate charts
Geographic data Map Chart / Choropleth Bar chart
Flow / proportion changes Sankey Diagram Pie chart

Design Principles

1. Data-Ink Ratio (Edward Tufte)

Maximize the proportion of your chart's "ink" that represents actual data. Remove:

  • Gridlines that aren't necessary
  • 3D effects (they distort perception)
  • Decorative borders
  • Redundant legends

2. Color Rules

  • Use red for negative/declining
  • Use green for positive/growing
  • Use grey for background/neutral comparisons
  • Use ONE highlight color, not a rainbow
  • Ensure accessibility for color-blind users (avoid red/green only)

3. The 5-Second Rule

A good chart should be understood within 5 seconds. If it takes longer, simplify.

4. Titles are Insights

Bad title: "Sales by Region"

Good title: "North Region Leads Sales — 40% Above National Average"

7.3 — Storytelling with Data

⌄

A data analyst's ultimate job is not to produce charts — it is to drive decisions. That requires storytelling.

The SCQA Framework (McKinsey)

Structure your presentation like a story:

  • Situation: What is the context? (background everyone agrees on)
  • Complication: What is the problem or challenge?
  • Question: What do we need to figure out?
  • Answer: Here is what the data shows — and what to do.

Example:

  • S: Our app has 500,000 monthly active users
  • C: User retention has dropped from 70% to 52% over 3 months
  • Q: Why are users leaving, and how do we stop it?
  • A: Data shows users who don't complete onboarding churn at 3× the rate. We recommend redesigning onboarding to completion in < 5 minutes.

Presenting to Non-Technical Audiences

1. Lead with the insight, not the methodology

2. Use plain language — avoid statistical jargon

3. Use one key chart per insight — not 10 charts at once

4. Always tie back to business impact (PKR, %, customers)

5. End with a clear recommendation — not just observations
